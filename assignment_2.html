Assignment 2: Reflections on Digital Wellbeing

Screen Time

Digital wellbeing is oftentimes tied to the metric of screen time, or the amount of time we spend looking at a screen like smartphones, computers, or TVs. Especially in the advent of COVID-19, many have become increasingly more concerned about excessive screen time since it was necessary to transition our lives online in the interest of public health. A review of the current literature on increased screen time during COVID-19 concluded that although “there are mixed consequences of prolonged screen time use and blurred understanding between healthy and unhealthy social connectedness over digital media, the suggestions for negative implications on (physical and) mental health warrant a strict need for inculcating healthy digital habits, especially knowing that digital technology is here to stay and grow with time.”

As our lives become more entangled with technology, our digital wellbeing cannot be summarized by simply the time we’ve spent on a screen. Devices are not just used in our free time for games, entertainment, and social networking. Many other facets of our life are now tied to screens for various productivity, educational, and lifestyle purposes.

Digital Habits

While there have been multiple research studies correlating screen time with physical and mental health issues, an editorial commentary published in JAMA Pediatrics reported that this association is “often inconsistent and, at best, small.”

“We should not assume that the amount of screen time is a singular factor in overall well-being.”
– Janis Whitlock, PhD, MPH and Philipp K. Masur, PhD

Screen time is not necessarily indicative of what we are doing on them. You could be learning a new skill by watching a YouTube video, connecting with your family and friends on FaceTime, or tracking your workout progress on Apple’s iOS Health app – all of which increase your screen time but are also related to improving your physical and mental health.

Our digital wellbeing suffers when we allow technology to compromise our lives offline. Recently, I have seen the term chronically online circulate on various social media platforms like Instagram, Twitter, and TikTok. According to urban dictionary, this term describes “someone who is basically always on the internet and their entire existence revolves around being on the internet.” Some individuals have pointed out that the “novel” experiences others are tweeting about are really a basic human experience, suggesting that they may be chronically online.

This discourse calls out individuals who spend so much time online that they lack awareness of the real world. Social media networks have been accused of manipulating users to spend more time on their apps and may be partially responsible for encouraging harmful digital habits that prevent users from “logging off.” Although tech companies should promote healthy engagement with their technology, we, as users, are not necessarily in control of how or when developers make these improvements. We are in control of how we choose to use technology. Digital wellbeing is unique to each person, and it is up to us to decide how what it means for ourselves to have a healthy engagement with technology.

Reflections

Personally, I do not find it useful to look at my screen time at all. I have found it more beneficial to define what healthy digital habits looks like in my life and acknowledge why I feel the need to use certain apps, not only for my school and work responsibilities, but my personal hobbies as well.

With this in mind, I feel less inclined to open apps that do not pertain to whatever I am focusing on in that moment. Rather than habitually scrolling on my phone in my free time, I only open an app once I’ve made the conscious decision to. This perspective has helped me acknowledge that technology is merely a tool to enhance my activities and remain grounded in the real world. I find that maintaining a healthy relationship with technology requires effort in understanding what it can provide for us while establishing boundaries.
